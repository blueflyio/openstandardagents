# Academia and Education (2025-2026)

## Cornell (eCornell): from LLM basics to agentic architecture

Cornell's Agentic AI Architecture certificate and related course material frame agent systems as an architecture discipline, not just prompt engineering. The curriculum emphasizes:
- LLM/API fundamentals,
- retrieval-augmented generation (RAG),
- advanced agentic workflows/multi-agent patterns,
- and governance/risk/security considerations for enterprise adoption [[R12]][[R13]].

This aligns with a practical "bridge" model: move from single-model interactions to orchestrated workflows with explicit control and policy boundaries [[R12]][[R13]].

## Harvard: protocol-centered institutional framing

Harvard's Library Innovation Lab and Berkman Klein Center "Agent Protocols Tech Tree" work argues that open protocols are a strategic governance lever for the agent ecosystem. Their framing is that protocols reveal what the builder community is standardizing in practice, similar to internet protocol formation dynamics [[R14]][[R15]].

Policy/legal discussion in the Harvard JOLT digest highlights institutional tension around agentic web governance: open infrastructure vs platform control over autonomous software behavior [[R53]].  

Interpretation: Harvard sources treat protocol design as governance infrastructure, not merely technical plumbing.

## MIT: deployment acceleration, transparency gaps, and risk realism

The MIT AI Agent Index (2025 edition) reports:
- strong release acceleration (24/30 tracked agents launched or materially updated in 2024-2025),
- rising autonomy levels,
- and a marked disclosure gap between capability claims and safety evidence [[R16]][[R17]][[R55]].

The Index also flags lack of established standards for agent behavior on the web [[R16]][[R17]].

MIT Sloan guidance for 2026 reinforces a cautious operational posture: hallucinations and prompt-injection risk remain material constraints; near-term adoption should keep humans in consequential loops and target reuse-friendly, bounded deployments [[R18]][[R19]].

## arXiv (security-focused)
Recent arXiv work shows an active shift toward:
- prompt-injection taxonomies,
- benchmark-driven defense comparisons,
- and causal/temporal mitigation methods in agent loops [[R54]].

This indicates the research frontier is moving from "is this a risk?" to measurable defense tradeoffs in realistic tool-using agents.

## Implications for research and teaching

Across Cornell, Harvard, and MIT materials:
1. **Interoperability and protocol literacy** are becoming core competencies.
2. **Security/governance controls** are now first-class design requirements, not "post-deployment hardening."
3. **Evaluation quality** (safety disclosure, benchmark transparency, operational evidence) is lagging capability marketing and needs tighter standards [[R16]][[R17]][[R49]].
