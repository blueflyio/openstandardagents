# Academia and education notes (2025-2026)

## 1) Cornell (applied curriculum orientation)

eCornell's Agentic AI Architecture program explicitly bridges:

- LLM behavior/prompting and context engineering,
- RAG implementation,
- multi-agent workflows/protocols (including MCP mention),
- governance, risk, security, and human oversight. [R08]

This is notable because it frames agentic AI as **systems engineering plus governance**, not just model prompting.

## 2) Harvard (protocol institutions and governance framing)

### Harvard Library Innovation Lab (APTT)

Harvard LIL's Agent Protocols Tech Tree argues open protocols are the most practical governance lever for a fast-moving agent ecosystem, drawing direct parallels to TCP/IP, DNS, and HTTP formation dynamics. [R09]

Core framing:

- protocols reveal what the builder community has converged on,
- protocol design influences capability boundaries,
- rough-consensus, running-code dynamics are reappearing for agent infrastructure. [R09]

### Harvard policy/legal writing on the "agentic web"

JOLT/Harvard policy writing emphasizes institutional questions around authority, accountability, and interoperable identity/delegation as agents begin acting across third-party platforms. [R13]

HKS Student Policy Review adds an infrastructure stress perspective:

- bot traffic is already around half of Internet traffic (with citation chains),
- agent-to-agent activity could scale faster than human traffic patterns,
- policy needs protocol-level support and stronger human-vs-bot verification mechanisms. [R14]

## 3) MIT (measurement and deployment realism)

### MIT AI Agent Index (2025 edition)

MIT's Index and companion paper provide one of the strongest structured snapshots of deployed agent systems:

- 30 indexed agents, annotated across product/accountability/capability/autonomy/ecosystem/safety dimensions. [R10][R11]
- A central finding: strong capability disclosure but weak safety transparency. [R10][R11]
- Reported in the paper/details pages: many systems lack disclosed internal or third-party safety evaluations. [R10][R11]

The paper also highlights ecosystem interaction issues (web conduct, identity/interoperability variance, anti-bot tension). [R11]

### MIT Sloan (operational guidance)

MIT Sloan's 2026 decision-maker guidance is explicitly cautious:

- ongoing hallucination and prompt-injection risks are slowing enterprise confidence,
- but some researchers/analysts still project major transaction handling by agents within ~5 years. [R12]

This produces a practical stance: aggressive experimentation, conservative autonomy.
