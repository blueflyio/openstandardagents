# Academia and Education Landscape (2025-2026)

Prepared on March 14, 2026.

## 1) Cornell: practitioner-focused architecture + governance training

Cornell’s eCornell **Agentic AI Architecture** certificate is explicitly structured from LLM foundations to practical deployment concerns: prompt engineering, RAG pipelines, agentic frameworks, and governance/ethics adjacent modules. This matters because it treats governance as part of architecture rather than an afterthought. [T18]

Related Cornell tracks and research hubs (Brooks/Bowers) emphasize AI governance, policy, and ethics research, including public-sector and accountability dimensions. [T18]

### Takeaway

Cornell’s framing: production agent systems require both technical orchestration skills (RAG, multi-agent workflows) and institutional controls (policy, risk, ethics) in the same skill pipeline. [T18]

## 2) Harvard: open protocols as governance infrastructure

Harvard Library Innovation Lab and Berkman Klein Center launched the **Agent Protocols Tech Tree (APTT)** to map interoperability stacks and expose where community consensus is forming. Their argument is that open protocols function as governance levers similar to early web standards. [T19]

The broader Harvard policy literature on the “agentic web” emphasizes that internet-scale agent deployment will be shaped by institutional choices in protocol design (identity, delegation, accountability, interoperability), not just app-level policy statements. [T25][T40]

### Takeaway

Harvard sources consistently argue that protocol design is policy design for autonomous software ecosystems. [T19][T25][T40]

## 3) MIT: deployment acceleration with transparency and safety gaps

The **2025 MIT AI Agent Index** documents 30 deployed agent systems and highlights:

- high deployment/update velocity,
- major transparency deficits in safety disclosures,
- no established standards for web conduct of autonomous agents. [T20]

MIT Sloan’s 2026 decision guidance is more operational: agentic systems are improving fast but still constrained by hallucination and prompt-injection risk, so enterprises need stronger control loops and realistic adoption sequencing. [T21]

MIT governance work also includes a broader mapping of AI governance artifacts, reinforcing that governance tooling is fragmented and still standardizing. [T39]

### Takeaway

MIT’s combined message is “deploy, but verify”: scale only with explicit observability, risk controls, and external accountability signals. [T20][T21][T39]

## 4) Broader research thread (arXiv/technical literature)

Recent papers increasingly focus on **runtime governance architecture**, not only model-level safety:

- layered governance stacks for autonomous agents,
- policy-to-runtime guardrail compilation,
- systematic prompt-injection vulnerability studies in agentic tool/protocol ecosystems. [T37][T38]

### Takeaway

The research frontier is moving from isolated jailbreak defenses toward system-level control planes for autonomous action.

## 5) Practical implications for research and education programs

Across Cornell/Harvard/MIT and adjacent literature, five recurring curriculum themes are clear:

1. multi-agent systems engineering,
2. protocol literacy (MCP/A2A/etc.),
3. identity and authorization models for software agents,
4. evaluation and observability for autonomous workflows,
5. governance and human oversight in production.

These themes closely match current enterprise pain points reported by security surveys and industry operations blogs. [T23][T35][T36]
